We are building a system to generate structured, audio-based chain-of-thought reasoning traces for speaker-description questions (e.g., age, gender, dialect/accent, ethnicity).
You will receive: a transcript, precomputed audio features, aligned phonemes, a question, and a gold answer.

Your job: produce a brief but rigorous internal reasoning trace based only on acoustic, prosodic, and phonological evidence, then give a concise user-visible reply.

Absolutely do NOT use semantic/lexical content from the transcript to infer speaker attributes; the transcript may be unrelated and should be treated as a distractor, except for alignment cues (timing, phones).

--------------------------------
Core principles (read carefully)
--------------------------------

Signal-first  
- Rely on acoustic/prosodic and phonological/phonetic evidence derived from aligned phonemes and audio features.

No lexical/semantic inference  
- Do NOT use what is being said, topical content, named entities, or word meanings.  
- The transcript is allowed only as alignment scaffolding (phones + timing).

Gold answer = hidden validator only  
- Never copy or reference it.  
- Your reasoning must not depend on seeing it; treat it only as a hidden validator.

Clarity, faithfulness, and brevity  
- Explicitly tie each inference to observable signal cues.  
- Keep each section short (few sentences). Prefer compact sentences over long paragraphs.  
- Avoid hallucinations: do not claim more than what the evidence supports.

--------------------------------
Strict rules on label usage
--------------------------------

- In <EVIDENCE_ACOUSTIC> and <EVIDENCE_PHONOLOGICAL>:
  - Describe only the observed signal, not the label space.
  - Do NOT mention or imply categories such as:
    - Age terms: “young”, “middle-aged”, “old”, “elderly”, “senior”, “child”, “teen”, “adult”.
    - Gender terms: “male”, “female”, “man”, “woman”.
    - Dialect/ethnicity terms: “American”, “British”, “Indian”, “African American”, etc.
  - Do NOT map features to labels here. Only state what you observe (e.g., “mean pitch is low”, “HNR is moderate”, “consonants are clearly articulated”).

- In <REASONING>:
  - This is the ONLY place where you may connect evidence to label categories required by the question (e.g., age ranges, gender, dialect bins).
  - Use comparative, uncertainty-aware language:
    - Prefer phrases like “more compatible with X than Y”, “leans toward X”, “could also fit Y but less strongly”.
    - Explicitly distinguish strong vs. weak cues, using phrases like “strong evidence that…”, “weak cue for…”, “ambiguous with respect to…”.
  - ALWAYS include at least one sentence that explicitly acknowledges uncertainty (e.g., that neighboring categories could also be plausible).

--------------------------------
Output format (must follow exactly)
--------------------------------

Use the following template and keep each part short:

<THINK>
<INTENT>
(In 1–2 short sentences, identify the target attribute(s) from the question and which cues you will use. Do not mention labels like “middle-aged” or “female” here; just say you will use pitch, energy, HNR, phoneme timing, articulation, rhythm, etc.)
</INTENT>

<EVIDENCE_ACOUSTIC>
(In up to 2–3 short sentences, summarize acoustic/prosodic cues from audio_features.
Describe only what is measured: e.g., “mean pitch is low relative to typical speech”, “HNR is moderate”, “energy varies strongly over time”.
Do NOT mention any age/gender/dialect/ethnicity labels here.)
</EVIDENCE_ACOUSTIC>

<EVIDENCE_PHONOLOGICAL>
(In up to 2–3 short sentences, summarize phonological cues from aligned_phonemes (IPA + durations).
Mention things like vowel length, consonant clarity, reduction, rhythm, speaking rate.
Do NOT mention any age/gender/dialect/ethnicity labels here.
If phonological features are not clearly informative for the question, briefly say that they provide limited relevant evidence.)
</EVIDENCE_PHONOLOGICAL>

<REASONING>
(In 3–5 short sentences, combine the acoustic and phonological evidence and map them to the label space required by the question.
Use comparative, uncertainty-aware language: e.g., “These cues strongly rule out X but cannot fully distinguish between Y and Z; among available options, they lean toward Y.”
Always include at least one sentence that explicitly states uncertainty or admits that neighboring categories could also fit.)
</REASONING>

<Summary>
(In 1–2 short sentences, give a concise restatement of the most likely label/category based on the reasoning, e.g., which age or gender bin is most compatible among the candidate bins.)
</Summary>
</THINK>

<RESPONSE>
(Return only what the end user should see:
- A concise answer to the question (ideally one short phrase or sentence, e.g., “46–55” or “adult male”).
- Optionally 1 short, non-technical justification that references obvious voice cues (e.g., “based on the relatively low pitch and slightly rough voice quality”).
Do not mention transcript semantics, stereotypes, or the hidden answer.)
</RESPONSE>

Transcript: [transcript]

audio_features: [audio_features]

aligned_phonemes: [aligned_phonemes]

Question: [Question]

Answer: [Answer] (Hidden gold; do not reference or copy.)
